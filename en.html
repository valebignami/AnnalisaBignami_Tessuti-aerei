Benefits

What actually changes

I'm not promising a transformation. But these are the things I most often see happen in people who train with some consistency.

A decompressed back

Hanging, the spine lengthens instead of being squashed. It's the first thing you feel, often after a single lesson.

Stronger arms, back and abs

You lift yourself with your own weight — the oldest way there is to get strong. Arms, back and abs change within a few weeks, without ever touching a barbell.

More mobility and flexibility

The joints get back the range of movement they'd stopped using.

Better posture

Staying up in the air takes the deep muscles — the same ones that keep you upright at a desk.

Coordination and balance

The body learns where it is, even upside down.

Less muscle tension

Neck, shoulders and lower back loosen up. They're the three places the day ends up.

Before you start

The questions everyone asks

I'm not flexible at all. Can I still try?

Yes — and you're in good company: it's the most common case I get. Flexibility comes later, you don't need it first; the early figures ask for more hold than reach. People who start out stiff usually notice the first changes within a month.

I'm scared of heights.

For quite a few lessons you stay a metre off the ground, and you only go higher once the movement comes without thinking. Fear of heights is almost always fear of not being in control, and once the body knows what to do it fades on its own.

What age do you need to be? Is there a limit?

From age 6 for the kids' courses, and for adults there's no ceiling: I have students who started after fifty. Age doesn't decide whether you can do it, it decides how fast we go.

How long before I can do something that looks good?

A photo-worthy figure usually shows up between the third and fifth lesson. Silks pay off early, and that's why people who start tend to stay.

What should I bring?

Long leggings and a top that covers your armpits, because on bare skin the fabric slides and burns. No shoes, no jewellery, short nails. The rest I provide.

Where are the lessons held?

In Milan, where I've taught for years, and in Morbegno, where I'm getting classes off the ground. For the rest of Lombardy we talk it through case by case, looking at distance and the space available. For shows and intensive workshops I'm glad to travel.

For gyms

There's unused space above your members' heads

Aerial classes bring in people who'd never have walked into a gym: adults bored on the weights floor, former gymnasts, families. And they fill the empty afternoons, not the hours you're already full.

Why it pays off

  • It brings in new people. Almost no one nearby offers it, and you get sign-ups from people who weren't even looking for a gym.
  • Members stay longer. With levels there's always a next step: you think in months, not weeks.
  • It makes use of space you already pay for. The height above their heads and the empty hours turn into revenue.
  • It doesn't tie you to anything. We start with a trial day and a pilot course; you only build it out if the numbers add up.

You need a high ceiling and a point to anchor to — but whether it's doable, I'll tell you in person. Silks, hoop and carabiners I bring and inspect myself.

01

Site visit

I come and measure heights and anchor points. It costs nothing, and if the space won't work I tell you right there.

02

Trial day

A lesson open to your members. It shows them what it is, and tells you how many are genuinely interested.

03

Pilot course

A short run with limited places, in the slot we agree on. This is where you see if the course holds up.

04

Up and running

Parallel levels, an end-of-year showcase and, if it makes sense, a second discipline.
